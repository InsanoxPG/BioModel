Circulatory system (Blood vessels)

The circulatory system consists of the heart, blood, and blood vessels and its purpose is to circulate hormones, nutrients, and other essential resources around the body. It mainly uses blood as a fluid to transport these resources around. In insects, spiders, and most mollusks, blood is replaced by hemolymph. Blood vessels and lymph vessels are the roads that blood travels through to get around the body. The heart is the center of the system, and it is the pump that initiates all movement. There are two types of circulatory systems, open and closed. The main difference is that open circulatory systems pump hemolymph into open cavities called hemocoel where they soak into tissues. In closed circulatory systems, the vessel system carries blood through isolated pathways before it diffuses into tissues through thin capillary walls. Some examples of organisms with open circulatory systems are insects, crustaceans, spiders, and mollusks(snails, clams, and oysters). Some examples of organisms with closed circulatory systems are vertebrates(mammals, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and fish), and some worms, octopi, and squid. One thing to note about open circulatory system organisms is that they are all small. Their size is limited by the open circulatory system because open systems are less efficient in transporting nutrients to tissues. The large amount of nutrients to be diffused to the tissue means that diffusion occurs much slower in open circulatory systems. In closed circulatory systems, the small amount of nutrients in each tiny capillary diffuses much faster into the tissue. There are three subclasses of vessels: arteries, capillaries, and veins. Arteries carry blood away from the heart, so the red blood cells contain a lot of oxygen. These are typically colored red in blood diagrams because of the high oxygen content in red blood cells. Arteries have to deal with the high-pressure blood being pumped from the heart, so they are thick walled and lined with smooth muscle to prevent blockage. Veins carry blood to the heart after the nutrients have dispersed into tissues. They contain little oxygen because it has been distributed throughout the body. They are typically colored blue in blood diagrams because of the lack of oxygen in red blood cells. Veins have thinner walls than arteries because there is less pressure further away from the heart. They also have valves to stop blood from flowing back because of gravity. The last type of vessel is the capillary. These vessels are incredibly thin, only letting red blood cells move through in a single file.

Circulatory System (Heart)

Humans have a four-chambered heart, and so do birds and other mammals. This allows for a complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. The sinoatrial node is nervous tissue that controls the timing of heartbeats. A perfectly timed impact on the heart during a specific phase of the heartbeat can stop the heart. The sinoatrial node causes atria to contract, and it sends a signal to the atrioventricular node to signal ventricles. Systolic pressure is blood pressure when the heart contracts, and diastolic pressure is blood pressure between heart beats. Systolic pressure is always higher than diastolic pressure because it is measured when the heart beats. Atria and ventricles make up the four chambers of the heart. The right atria and right ventricle receive and pump blood into the lungs, and the left atria and left ventricle receive and pump blood into the body. Remember that these directions are from a standard anatomical view. The atriums fill with blood and pass it to the ventricles, meaning atriums are superior to ventricles.

Circulatory System (Blood)

Blood is classified as specialized connective tissue that transports oxygen, nutrients and waste (CO2) around the body. Blood consists of 55% plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. Red blood cells contain hemoglobin to carry oxygen, and they lack a nucleus. This means that they cannot reproduce. They forgo a nucleus because it allows for more room for hemoglobin and thus more oxygen carrying capability. Red blood cells are constantly being produced in the bone marrow via a process called hematopoiesis. White blood cells are part of the immune system. Platelets are cell fragments that facilitate blood clotting (hemostasis).
